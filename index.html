QAnon is treated here as an object of study, not a set of claims to be entertained. Every load-bearing statement carries an epistemic tag (see the rail), debunked assertions are flagged with rebuttals, and the conspiracy's own texts appear only as primary-source discourse objects for analysis. The aim is calibration: to say clearly what is documented, what is contested, and what is false — and, in the computational sections, to measure structure in the corpus without ever treating the maths as a verdict on truth.

What this is — and the rules it follows

Document and analyse; never amplify or endorse

Purpose & ethics. This project documents and analyses QAnon for academic, journalistic, and educational use. It does not promote its beliefs. Conspiracy assertions are labelled as such; debunked claims (e.g. the blood-libel-descended "adrenochrome" myth) are marked and rebutted; the drop texts are reproduced only as artifacts for analysis, with referenced images deliberately excluded; and survivor-support framing is carried throughout. The computational pages add a second discipline on top of this: every clustering result ships with validity numbers and a stability check, and is explicitly bounded by what the method cannot see.

Two ideas run through everything. First, the epistemic tags in the rail: each claim is graded from Documented down to Conspiracy, so the reader always knows the evidential status of a statement. Second, calibration over certainty: where credible sources disagree (how large is the real-world threat? who actually wrote the drops?), the dossier presents the disagreement rather than resolving it by fiat — most sharply on the Critical Review page.

How the drop analysis was built

Each component, what it does, and where it lives

The Drops lab is two Python scripts plus an interactive page. qdrops_cluster.py turns the raw archive into one analysed dataset; qdrops_sweep.py runs many configurations and scores their agreement; drops.html reads the output and renders it. Here is each piece.

  1. 1 · Topic clustering — letting themes self-form

    Embeddings → UMAP → HDBSCAN (not k-NN)

    Each drop is converted into a sentence-embedding vector that captures meaning, not just keywords. The vectors are compressed with UMAP and grouped with HDBSCAN, a density-based clusterer that chooses the number of topics itself and sets genuinely odd drops aside as "noise." BERTopic wraps this and labels each topic by its most distinctive words (class-based TF-IDF, with stopwords removed so labels read like "covid 19 virus lockdown," not "the of to"). Why not k-NN? k-NN is a supervised classifier — it needs predefined labels and so cannot discover topics; it only enters clustering legitimately as a nearest-neighbour graph fed to community detection. A plain TF-IDF + KMeans baseline is also included for comparison.

  2. 2 · Validity — measuring whether the clustering is any good

    Silhouette + c_v topic coherence

    "Good clustering" is measured, not eyeballed. Every run reports a silhouette score (how cleanly separated the clusters are, −1…1) and a c_v topic-coherence score from gensim (how interpretable each topic's words are, 0…1), both overall and per topic. Crucially, these are validity, not truth: a high score means the algorithm grouped the text well — it says nothing about whether any claim in the drops is correct.

  3. 3 · Stability — trusting only the topics that persist

    Multi-run sweep + membership-overlap (Jaccard)

    Clustering is parameter-sensitive: change the embedding model or the minimum cluster size and the topics shift. So the sweep runs several configurations (e.g. MiniLM vs. MPNet × min-cluster-size 15/25/40), embedding once per model for speed. Because every run clusters the same drops, topics can be matched across runs by document-membership overlap (Jaccard). A topic's persistence is its mean best-overlap with the other runs: stable (≥0.50) topics are probably real; fragile (<0.30) ones are likely artifacts of the settings. On the Drops page you can click any topic in the Stability panel to read its drops in the reader.

  4. 4 · Metadata mining — the fields most analyses discard

    Composition · board migration · tripcode rotations

    Beyond the text, each post carries structure worth charting. Composition shows how much of the corpus is actually prose vs. image-only or bare references — a quantitative footprint of vagueness. Board migration plots where drops were posted, by year, revealing the 4chan → 8chan → 8kun platform diaspora the literature tracks. And the tripcode-rotation timeline shows each cryptographic signature Q posted under and when it changed: rotations are documented authenticity fault-lines (every handoff is a point where account control could change), and secure "!!" tripcodes are distinguished from the brute-forceable single-"!" ones.

  5. 5 · Administrative tagging — separating ops from ideology

    Heuristic operational-drop filter

    A large share of drops are operational, not ideological — "test," "trip update," "comms," "disregard spelling," board/config messages. A rule-based tagger flags these so they can be excluded at clustering time (--drop-admin) or hidden in the reader, sharpening the thematic picture. It's a transparent heuristic, not a classifier, and is labelled as such.

  6. 6 · Cross-reference — triangulating against the scholarship

    This corpus vs. the peer-reviewed findings

    The honest move is to check the computed results against independent work and to be explicit about blind spots. A table on the Drops page lines up this corpus's measured facts with published conclusions — the Gospel/iDRAMA paper, de Zeeuw's normiefication study, Hoseini's Telegram analysis, Priniski's good-vs-evil mental-model paper — and marks each Consistent, Adjacent, Out-of-scope, or Needs-stylometry. That calibration — saying what the method can and cannot see — is the analytical payload.

Data & reproducibility

Where the drops come from, and how to regenerate everything

Source datasetResearch only

The corpus is the open JSON-QAnon archive (all known drops through Nov 2022), whose own author prefaces it: "QAnon is a dangerous cult… for research purposes only." Cite it as Kingsman, J. (2025), JSON-QAnon, DOI 10.13140/RG.2.2.28778.32964. The drops are reproduced here strictly as primary-source artifacts; referenced images are excluded.

The whole lab is reproducible from the command line. The pipeline downloads the dataset automatically:

pip install -r requirements.txt python qdrops_cluster.py --inspect # see the data shape (optional) python qdrops_cluster.py --reduce-outliers # one clustering, with validity scores python qdrops_sweep.py # several configs + stability_report.json

This writes qdrops_clustered.json (and, from the sweep, run_*.json + stability_report.json). Place those next to drops.html and reload — or use the in-page file-pickers. Full code, methods, and citations live in the repository.

How to read the analytics — and what they can't show

The same calibration the Critical Review argues, applied to the maths

Three cautions travel with every chart in the Drops lab:

  1. Validity is not truth

    Silhouette and coherence measure whether the algorithm grouped the text sensibly. They are wholly silent on whether any drop's claims are accurate. A crisp, coherent "the storm / arrests" cluster says people repeated that theme — not that arrests occurred.

  2. Topics are provisional

    Cluster boundaries move with the embedding model, the minimum cluster size, and UMAP settings. That is exactly why the Stability panel exists: treat a topic as real only if it persists across configurations, and read the noise share as a measure of how much of the corpus resists grouping at all.

  3. The method has blind spots

    Clustering measures themes and timing. It cannot establish authorship (that needs stylometry — see the "who is Q" debate on the Critical Review), it does not measure toxicity (that needs a trained classifier), and it cannot see the movement's non-English, off-platform life (the Telegram/global corpora). Findings outside its reach are flagged, not fudged.

Read this way, the lab's value is not a single answer but a structured, checkable reading of the corpus — one that states its own confidence and its own limits, in the same spirit as the rest of the dossier.

One project, two disciplines. The written pages grade every claim by evidence; the computational pages grade every result by validity and stability. Neither treats QAnon's assertions as true. Together they aim to model what careful, transparent analysis of a conspiracy movement looks like — documenting and explaining it without ever amplifying it.
