The Report

A short operator's guide to this project: how to read it and how to run it, an honest account of what it contains and what it actually reveals once the Methods Audit is applied, and a novel closing argument that connects the lab's core finding to the news of mid-2026 — the stalled Epstein-files release and the AI tools now dressing conspiracy up as "data analysis."

This site is two things welded together: a fully-cited dossier on QAnon as a political movement and a computational lab that treats 4,966 "Q drops" as data. This page is the map for both — and, at the end, the one conclusion the whole project was built to earn the right to make.

How to use it, step by step

Two paths: read the argument, or re-run the analysis yourself

Path A — the reader. You want the argument and the evidence, not a Python environment. Follow the pages in this order:

  • Start on Home for the rules of reading

    It explains the epistemic tags (Documented / Reported / Alleged / Disputed / Debunked / Conspiracy) and how the analytics are meant to be read. Two minutes here keeps every later number in its proper box.

  • Read the Methods Audit before trusting any figure

    This is the unusual step and the important one. It tells you exactly which claims survived scrutiny and which did not, so you never over-read a chart. Treat it as the lens for everything else.

  • Work through the Dossier, then the Critical Review

    The Dossier lays out origin, beliefs, money, violence, and the cult-framework scorecards; the Review stress-tests them and rates each major claim Strong / Moderate / Contested. Read them as claim and counter-claim.

  • Open the Drops explorer to see the corpus directly

    The interactive cluster map, searchable reader, circadian and migration charts, and the Stability panel let you inspect the actual drops behind the prose. Click a stable topic to read its members.

  • Finish on Results + Future for the quantitative claims

    Results holds the clustering scoreboard and BITE numbers; Future carries the novel Delegated-Closure theory and its honest scorecard (what passed, what was rejected). The Timeline places it all against real-world events through 2026.

Path B — the analyst. You want to reproduce the numbers. Everything is deterministic and runs from the committed data; the only network step is the optional dataset download. From the project folder:

pip install -r requirements.txt # 1 · cluster the real Q corpus (auto-downloads posts.json) python qdrops_cluster.py --inspect # sanity-check the data shape first python qdrops_cluster.py # embeddings -> UMAP -> HDBSCAN + validity # 2 · don't trust one clustering — sweep and keep what PERSISTS python qdrops_sweep.py # writes stability_report.json # 3 · mine the metadata the topic pipeline discards python qdrops_patterns.py # circadian / style / cadence findings # 4 · the novel metric: per-drop interpretive gap-leaving python dci.py # writes results/improved/dci_findings.json # 5 · serve the site and read your own output python -m http.server 8009 # -> http://localhost:8009

The cross-platform scripts (collect_datasets.py, multi_dataset_analysis.py, compare_results.py) run too, but — per the Audit — six of their seven datasets are synthetic placeholders until real downstream corpora are collected (roadmap Phase 0). Run them to see the machinery; don't cite their numbers. For a paper-style ablation with plots, improved_experiment.py bundles the lot (T4-GPU Colab one-liner in the README).

What it reveals

Sorted by what survives the audit and what does not

Findings that hold — all computed on the real corpus and reproducible from the committed JSON:

  • Holds  A single human clock. Posting time-of-day collapses into a US-timezone band; the deepest 3-hour window (11–13 UTC) holds just 0.44% of drops — the sleep band for every US zone. 25.6% of consecutive drops are <5 min apart (typing sessions). Authorship stays unresolved; the clock does not.
  • Holds  Style thins across the migration. 4chan→8chan→8kun: median length 17→12→8 words, URLs 2.8%→31%→56%, questions/drop 3.22→1.21→1.20. The drops got shorter, vaguer, and more link-driven.
  • Holds  Two robust topics, one affect blob. At the coarsest setting the corpus is a slogan cluster plus a ~4,000-drop "god / patriots / time" affect mass; two independent models agree at Jaccard 0.928. (Caveat: this is robustness, not external proof — the scoring favours coarse splits.)
  • Holds  Delegated Closure is measurable. The DCI — a per-drop, LLM-free score of how much interpretive closure a drop withholds — is non-degenerate (mean 0.329), weight-invariant, and verified non-circular (zero lexicon overlap with the BITE scorer, partial-r ≈ −0.05). The highest-scoring drops are literal "Coincidence? Q" cascades; the lowest are URL-grounded link dumps.

Claims that do not hold — kept on the page on purpose, because burying them would break the contract:

  • Falls  The "5.6–7.1× downstream amplification." Blocked: the six downstream corpora are synthetic, so the cross-platform BITE gradient is a lexicon-and-authorship artifact, not audience manufacture.
  • Falls  The iDRAMA "diaspora" replication. Circular: each platform's first-appearance month equals a hard-coded constant the script injected.
  • Falls  "Openness rises over time." Downgraded: the drift is +0.0012/yr at permutation p=0.54 — indistinguishable from noise. It is a mode-shift, not a trend. (And the falsifiability-economy ratio actually fell.)
The net read. The drops are an affect-heavy, low-concreteness, vagueness-maximising stream on a US clock — and, crucially, a corpus engineered to be re-interpreted. That last property is the one that matters next, because it does not need Q to keep working.

A 2026 conclusion: the closure machine outlived its author

Where the lab's central finding meets this year's news

The last verified Q drop was posted in 2022. By the project's own measure, that should not have mattered — and in 2026 it plainly has not. The lab's load-bearing finding is Delegated Closure: the drops never transmitted a belief system, they withheld closure so the audience would author the missing meaning and own it as personal discovery. A stream optimised for that does not depend on its author. It depends on a steady supply of gaps. In 2026 the gap has a new name.

The Epstein files are a DCI-maximal object

Score the 2026 Epstein-files release on the same six components the DCI uses on the drops and it lights up every one: bracketed redactions, withheld names, a partial release that resolves nothing, and a steady drip of "why is this blacked out?" The single highest-DCI drop in the entire corpus is a "Coincidence? Q" cascade — and the 2026 news cycle ran on exactly that grammar, down to the genuinely-true, genuinely-meaningless detail that Epstein had met 4chan's founder around the launch of /pol/, the board where Q first appeared. A real coincidence, processed by a movement trained for years to read coincidence as cipher. The audience supplied the closure the documents withheld. That is the producer/consumer machine running with no producer at all.

Reporting: NPR, Feb 2026 · OPB, Jan 2026 — a partial release breeding more theories and less trust; QAnon "moving the goalposts" to claim vindication; a 107% rise in adjacent conspiracy mentions.

The dark mirror: "platform conspiracism"

The most unsettling 2026 development for a project like this one is that conspiracy theorists are now building AI interfaces to the Epstein files and presenting their conclusions as data analysis. Tools like WEBB — "a slick interface with literal red threads," promising to turn three million documents into "structured, searchable intelligence" — perform exactly the gestures this lab performs: embeddings, search, clustering, graphs, the aesthetic of rigor. Researchers have a name for it now: "platform conspiracism," a "conspiracy of data" in which misleading claims are laundered through charts until the polish reads as proof. Strip the labels off a WEBB screenshot and the Drops dashboard, and a casual visitor cannot tell which one is the conspiracy.

Analysis: The Conversation, May 2026.

So what actually separates them

Not the tools — both sides have the same embeddings and the same red thread. The difference is one page. A platform-conspiracist dashboard never ships an audit that says "six of our seven datasets are fake, our headline multiplier is an artifact, and our 'rising openness' is statistical noise at p=0.54." It cannot, because its purpose is to manufacture closure, not to withhold judgment until the evidence earns it. This project's only durable defense — the only thing that makes it a study of the closure machine rather than another node in it — is that it grades its own claims by evidence and publishes what fails. In an information environment where AI makes the appearance of analysis nearly free, that willingness to refute yourself is no longer a footnote of good methodology. It is the entire distinction between research and recruitment.

The conclusion, in one line. Q is gone and the machine he built is healthier than ever, because the scarce resource was never the drops, the platform, or even the author — it was the gap, and 2026 has an endless supply. The defense was never better data or better models, both of which the conspiracists now have too. The defense is adversarial self-verification — the discipline of the Audit page, applied relentlessly, in public. A dossier and a conspiracy can look identical right up until one of them shows you what it got wrong.
