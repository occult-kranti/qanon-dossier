The Literature

What the refereed record says about QAnon — anchored by the iDRAMA Lab's seven-year computational study of the movement's platform diaspora, and extended across security studies, psychology, religion, and diffusion research.

This page is organised into five thematic tracks rather than a single list, because QAnon scholarship has matured into distinct conversations that rarely cite across their own boundaries. Within each track the papers run chronologically, so the development of that conversation is visible. The spine of the collection is Track A — the body of work from the iDRAMA Lab (Blackburn, De Cristofaro, Stringhini, Zannettou, Papasavva and collaborators), whose seven-year, multi-platform measurement programme produced the canonical "Gospel According to Q" study and most of the hard data the field relies on. Books and institutional reports are included where they are field-defining, but are clearly flagged as not peer-reviewed.

A

The iDRAMA Lab computational corpus

Seven years tracking QAnon's platform diaspora — 4chan → Voat → Parler → Telegram → Poal

Why this track is the spineMethod note

The same research group followed QAnon across every platform it touched, building large public datasets at each step. Read in sequence, these papers are effectively one longitudinal study of how a conspiracy movement survives repeated deplatforming by migrating — and what it loses each time it moves.

  1. 2017A·1

    Kek, Cucks, and God Emperor Trump: A Measurement Study of 4chan's Politically Incorrect Forum and Its Effects on the Web

    Gabriel Hine, Jeremiah Onaolapo, Emiliano De Cristofaro, Nicolas Kourtellis, Ilias Leontiadis, Gianluca Stringhini & Jeremy Blackburn

    ConferenceICWSM 201711th Int'l AAAI Conf. · best-paper runner-up2017Peer-reviewed

    The foundational study of 4chan's /pol/ — the board where the first Q drop would appear months later. It characterises /pol/'s culture, raiding behaviour and outsized influence on the wider web. Essential background: QAnon did not emerge from nowhere, but from a community this paper had already mapped.

    Computational social sciencearXiv:1610.03452
  2. 2020A·2

    Raiders of the Lost Kek: 3.5 Years of Augmented 4chan Posts from the Politically Incorrect Board

    Antonis Papasavva, Savvas Zannettou, Emiliano De Cristofaro, Gianluca Stringhini & Jeremy Blackburn

    ConferenceICWSM 202014th Int'l AAAI Conf.2020Peer-reviewed

    Releases a dataset of ~134M /pol/ posts over 3.5 years, the empirical substrate for much subsequent work on Q's birthplace. The "augmentation" adds toxicity and entity labels, enabling large-scale study of how Q-related discourse incubated on 4chan before migrating outward.

    Dataset · 4chanarXiv:2001.07487
  3. 2021A·3

    A Large Open Dataset from the Parler Social Network

    Max Aliapoulios, Emmi Bevensee, Jeremy Blackburn, Emiliano De Cristofaro, Gianluca Stringhini & Savvas Zannettou

    ConferenceICWSM 202115th Int'l AAAI Conf.2021Peer-reviewed

    A dataset of ~183M Parler posts by ~4M users (Aug 2018–Jan 2021), capturing the platform that became a QAnon and "Stop the Steal" hub in the run-up to January 6. Documents the influx of users after high-profile endorsements and the spike around the 2020 election.

    Dataset · ParlerarXiv:2101.03820
  4. 2021A·4

    "Is it a Qoincidence?": An Exploratory Study of QAnon on Voat

    Antonis Papasavva, Jeremy Blackburn, Gianluca Stringhini, Savvas Zannettou & Emiliano De Cristofaro

    ConferenceThe Web Conference (WWW '21)30th WWW2021Peer-reviewed

    After Reddit banned QAnon, much of the community moved to Voat. This is the first large-scale measurement of that refuge — hundreds of thousands of submissions and comments from /v/GreatAwakening — characterising the discourse against a platform baseline.

    Web measurement · VoatarXiv:2009.04885
  5. 2022A·5 ★

    The Gospel According to Q: Understanding the QAnon Conspiracy from the Perspective of Canonical Information

    Max Aliapoulios, Antonis Papasavva, Cameron Ballard, Emiliano De Cristofaro, Gianluca Stringhini, Savvas Zannettou & Jeremy Blackburn

    ConferenceICWSM 2022Proc. 16th Int'l AAAI Conf., 16(1) 735–7462022Peer-reviewed

    The most-cited technical study of the drops, and the analytical centre of this whole collection. The authors build a dataset of 4,949 canonical Q drops from six aggregation sites, show the sites disagree on what is canonical (so some drops are likely apocryphal), and — comparing writing across the most-used tripcodes — find statistically significant evidence the drops were not authored by a single individual. They also find the drops are not especially toxic in themselves: the violence comes from adherents' interpretations.

    Canonical analysis · Stylometrydoi.org/10.1609/icwsm.v16i1.19330arXiv:2101.08750
  6. 2023A·6

    On the Globalization of the QAnon Conspiracy Theory Through Telegram

    Mohamad Hoseini, Philipe Melo, Fabrício Benevenuto, Anja Feldmann & Savvas Zannettou

    ConferenceACM WebSci 2023Proc. 15th ACM Web Science Conf., 75–852023Peer-reviewed

    The first multilingual study of QAnon, analysing 4.5M messages across 161 Telegram groups/channels. A striking finding: after 2020, German overshadows English in these channels — concrete evidence that QAnon became a genuinely global, and especially European, phenomenon rather than a purely American one.

    Multilingual · Telegramdoi.org/10.1145/3578503.3583603arXiv:2105.13020
  7. 2024A·7

    Waiting for Q: An Exploration of QAnon Users' Online Migration to Poal in the Wake of Voat's Demise

    Antonis Papasavva & Enrico Mariconti

    ConferenceACM WebSci 202416th ACM Web Science Conf.2024Peer-reviewed

    The latest chapter in the diaspora. When Voat shut down (Dec 2020), QAnon users migrated again — this time to Poal. The paper tracks that migration, closing the loop on a seven-year record of a community that responds to every ban by relocating rather than dissolving.

    Platform migration · Poaldoi.org/10.1145/3614419.3644021
B

Radicalization, violence & security

Is QAnon a security threat, a radical idea collective, or both?

  1. 2020B·1

    The QAnon Conspiracy Theory: A Security Threat in the Making?

    Amarnath Amarasingam & Marc-André Argentino · Queen's University / Concordia University

    JournalCTC Sentinel (West Point)Vol. 13, No. 7, 37–442020Terrorism studies

    The agenda-setting security analysis. Surveying QAnon-linked criminal cases — including one terrorism guilty plea — the authors argue QAnon represents a genuine public-security threat, and warn that the interval between radicalization and mobilization can be as short as days. (CTC Sentinel is editorially reviewed rather than blind peer-reviewed, but is a flagship terrorism-studies venue.)

    Security & terrorism studiesCTC Sentinel
  2. 2021B·2

    QAnon Conspiracy Theory: Examining its Evolution and Mechanisms of Radicalization

    Amanda Garry, Samantha Walther, Rukaya Mohamed & Ayan Mohammed

    JournalJournal for DeradicalizationNo. 26 (Spring), 152–2162021Peer-reviewed

    A long, systematic treatment of how QAnon recruits and radicalises, framing it as a decentralised "big-tent" ideology whose gamified participation draws ordinary people into an extremist worldview. One of the most thorough peer-reviewed accounts of QAnon's radicalization mechanics.

    Deradicalization studiesJournal for Deradicalization
  3. 2021B·3

    QAnon-Inspired Violence in the United States: An Empirical Assessment of a Misunderstood Threat

    Michael A. Jensen & Sheehan Kane · START, University of Maryland

    JournalBehavioral Sciences of Terrorism & Political AggressionTaylor & Francis2021Peer-reviewed

    An empirical assessment of QAnon-inspired offenders that complicates easy narratives in both directions — documenting a real violence nexus while pushing back on threat-inflation. A key data-driven counterweight to purely qualitative accounts.

    Empirical · Violence
  4. 2021B·4

    QAnon: Radical Opinion versus Radical Action

    Sophia Moskalenko & Clark McCauley

    JournalPerspectives on TerrorismVol. 15, No. 2, 142–1462021Peer-reviewed

    Applies McCauley & Moskalenko's "two-pyramids" model to argue that most QAnon believers hold radical opinions without taking radical action — a crucial distinction for calibrating the threat. This is the scholarly anchor of the "don't over-inflate the danger" position examined on the Critical Review page.

    Radicalization theory
  5. 2023B·5

    QAnon Beliefs, Political Radicalization and Support for January 6th Insurrection: A Gendered Perspective

    Sophia Moskalenko, Tomislav Pavlović & Brett Burton

    JournalTerrorism and Political ViolenceVol. 36, No. 7 · T&F2023Peer-reviewed

    A survey of 429 US participants linking QAnon belief to support for the January 6 riot and to Big-Five personality traits, with attention to gender. Connects belief measurement directly to the movement's most consequential real-world event.

    Survey · Gender · J6doi.org/10.1080/09546553.2023.2236230
C

Psychology & relational harm

Who joins, why it's sticky, and what it does to families

  1. 2021C·1

    Pastels and Pedophiles: Inside the Mind of QAnon

    Mia Bloom & Sophia Moskalenko

    MonographStanford University Press243 pp.2021

    The field's defining book. Bloom and Moskalenko trace QAnon from 4chan to Facebook and analyse how it drew in its largest segment — middle-class white women — through gamification: clues, the dopamine of "solving," and a sense of community and purpose that became addictive. (A scholarly monograph, not a peer-reviewed paper, but foundational and richly sourced.)

    Psychology · Monograph
  2. 2021C·2

    Characterizing Social Imaginaries and Self-Disclosures of Dissonance in Online Conspiracy Discussion Communities

    Shruti Phadke, Mattia Samory & Tanushree Mitra

    ConferenceProc. ACM HCI (CSCW)Vol. 5, CSCW22021Peer-reviewed

    Studies doubt rather than belief: it characterises moments when conspiracy participants disclose cognitive dissonance — signals of who may be starting to disengage. The same group's companion paper ("What makes people join conspiracy communities?") models the social pull in the other direction.

    HCI · CSCW
  3. 2022C·3

    Secondhand Conspiracy Theories: The Social, Emotional and Political Tolls on Loved Ones of QAnon Followers

    Sophia Moskalenko, Brett Burton, Juncal Fernández-Garayzábal González & Mia Bloom

    JournalDemocracy and SecurityVol. 19, No. 3, 231–2502022Peer-reviewed

    Quantifies the collateral damage: the anxiety, grief and relationship strain borne by family members of QAnon believers. Among the first peer-reviewed studies to treat the people around a believer as the unit of harm.

    Psychology · Harmdoi.org/10.1080/17419166.2022.2111305
  4. 2022C·4

    QAnon, Women, and the American Culture Wars

    Mia Bloom & Sophia Moskalenko

    JournalSocial Research: An International QuarterlyVol. 89, No. 3, 525–5502022Peer-reviewed

    Develops the gendered analysis from Pastels and Pedophiles: how "Save the Children" framing and wellness/"pastel QAnon" aesthetics recruited women, situating QAnon within longer American culture-war dynamics.

    Gender · Culturedoi.org/10.1353/sor.2022.0037
  5. 2023C·5

    "I Would Give Anything to Talk about Aliens Now": QAnon Conspiracy Theories and the Creation of Cognitive Deviance

    Meggan M. Jordan & Jennifer Marie Whitmer

    JournalSociological Perspectives (SAGE)2023Peer-reviewed

    Twenty in-depth interviews with adults worried about a family member's QAnon involvement, developing the concept of cognitive deviance — the point at which beliefs become deviant in the eyes of intimates. Empirical grounding for the dossier's survivor sections.

    Sociologydoi.org/10.1177/07311214231181383
  6. 2023C·6

    Qollateral: The Impact of QAnon on Loved Ones and the Potential for P/CVE Programs to Help

    Meghan St-Amant, Michael King, Lily Stolte & Marc-André Argentino

    JournalJournal for DeradicalizationNo. 35, 33–752023Peer-reviewed

    Moves from documenting harm to intervention: it asks whether the toolkit of Preventing/Countering Violent Extremism (P/CVE) can be adapted to help the families of QAnon believers — a rare bridge from analysis to applied response.

    P/CVE · InterventionJournal for Deradicalization
  7. 2024C·7

    "I one-hundred thousand percent blame it on QAnon": The Impact of QAnon Belief on Interpersonal Relationships

    Lauren Mastroni & Robyn Mooney

    JournalJ. of Social and Personal Relationships (SAGE)2024Peer-reviewed

    A recent qualitative study of how QAnon belief fractures close relationships, extending the relational-harm literature with first-person accounts and thematic analysis. Confirms that the interpersonal toll is consistent, severe, and under-served by support resources.

    Relationships · Qualitativedoi.org/10.1177/02654075241246124

Religion, ritual & meaning-making

QAnon as satanic-panic descendant, hyper-real religion, and pseudo-Christian movement

  1. 2020D·1

    QAnon as a Variation of a Satanic Conspiracy Theory: An Overview

    Miroslav Vrzal · Masaryk University

    JournalTheory and Practice in English StudiesVol. 9, No. 1–2, 45–662020Peer-reviewed

    Places QAnon in the lineage of satanic-panic narratives — the 1980s–90s ritual-abuse scare, blood-libel motifs, Manichaean good/evil framing. A corrective to treating QAnon as historically unprecedented: it inherits a recognisable conspiratorial grammar.

    Religious studies
  2. 2023D·2

    The Revelations of Q: Dissemination and Resonance of QAnon among US Evangelical Christians and the Role of the Covid-19 Crisis

    Heiko Beyer & Niklas Herrberg · Heinrich Heine University Düsseldorf

    JournalZ. für Religion, Gesellschaft und Politik (Springer)2023Peer-reviewed

    An empirical test of why QAnon found purchase among US evangelicals. Using Pew's American Trends Panel, the authors argue that biblicism breeds belief in absolute truth (nomization), a clean good/evil split (Manichaeism), and salvation through politics (immanent eschatology) — which under COVID uncertainty resonate with the pulls of conspiracy thinking. Holds when controlling for Trump support.

    Sociology of religiondoi.org/10.1007/s41682-023-00147-2
  3. 2025D·3

    "Stand Against the Wiles of the Devil": Interpreting QAnon as a Pseudo-Christian Extremist Movement

    Nicolò Miotto & Julian Droogan · Macquarie University

    JournalCritical Sociology (SAGE)2025Peer-reviewed

    A recent hermeneutic analysis of 121 religious images in QAnon discourse, arguing the movement operates as a pseudo-Christian extremist formation. Extends the "QAnon as religion" thread — alongside Argentino's influential framing of QAnon as a hyper-real religion akin to Heaven's Gate or Jediism.

    Religion · Extremismdoi.org/10.1177/08969205241228744

Belief, diffusion & method

How it spread, why disconfirmation doesn't kill it, and tools for studying it

  1. 2020E·1

    Tracing Normiefication: A Cross-Platform Analysis of the QAnon Conspiracy Theory

    Daniël de Zeeuw, Sal Hagen, Stijn Peeters & Emilija Jokubauskaitė · University of Amsterdam (OILab)

    JournalFirst MondayVol. 25, No. 112020Peer-reviewed

    The early cross-platform map. Tracing activity from 28 Oct 2017 to 1 Nov 2018, it theorises QAnon's diffusion as normiefication — fringe ideas crossing into mainstream platforms — with a clear 4chan incubation, rapid migration to YouTube and Reddit, and news coverage that lagged until the phenomenon moved offline.

    Media studies · Diffusiondoi.org/10.5210/fm.v25i11.10643
  2. 2021E·2

    Rise of QAnon: A Mental Model of Good and Evil Stews in an Echochamber

    J. Hunter Priniski, Mason McClay & Keith J. Holyoak · UCLA

    ConferenceCognitive Science Society (CogSci)Annual Meeting Proceedings2021Peer-reviewed

    Models QAnon belief as a coherent good-versus-evil mental model that gains explanatory power by absorbing new events, reinforced in an echo chamber. Helps explain why failed predictions rarely dislodge believers — the model simply re-absorbs the contradiction.

    Cognitive science
  3. 2021E·3

    Panic, Pizza and Mainstreaming the Alt-right: A Social Media Analysis of Pizzagate and the Rise of the QAnon Conspiracy

    Paul Bleakley

    JournalCurrent Sociology (SAGE)2021Peer-reviewed

    Traces the line from Pizzagate (2016) to QAnon, arguing the earlier panic was a proof-of-concept that helped mainstream alt-right framings — connecting the Comet Ping Pong shooting to the narrative machinery QAnon later industrialised.

    Sociology
  4. 2022E·4

    Paved with Bad Intentions: QAnon's Save the Children Campaign

    Cody Buntain, Martin Innes, Tamar Mitts & Jacob Shapiro

    JournalJournal of Online Trust and SafetyVol. 1, No. 22022Peer-reviewed

    An empirical study of the #SaveTheChildren hijack — how QAnon co-opted a child-safety hashtag to launder conspiracy content into mainstream audiences. Quantifies one of the movement's most effective mainstreaming tactics.

    Trust & safetydoi.org/10.54501/jots.v1i2.51
  5. 2024E·5

    Identifying and Characterizing Behavioral Classes of Radicalization within the QAnon Conspiracy on Twitter

    (Emily) L. Wang, Luca Luceri, Francesco Pierri & Emilio Ferrara · USC

    ConferenceICWSM 202418th Int'l AAAI Conf.2024Peer-reviewed

    Uses large-scale Twitter data to distinguish behavioral classes of QAnon engagement — separating committed propagators from peripheral or transient participants. A more granular alternative to treating "QAnon supporters" as a single undifferentiated bloc.

    Computational · Radicalization
  6. 2024E·6

    MentalManip: A Dataset for Fine-grained Analysis of Mental Manipulation in Conversations

    Yuxin Wang, Ivory Yang, Saeed Hassanpour & Soroush Vosoughi · Dartmouth College

    ConferenceACL 2024Proc. 62nd Annual Meeting of the ACL, 3747–37642024Peer-reviewed

    The methodological backbone for analysing manipulation as discourse: a 4,000-dialogue dataset annotated for manipulation techniques and the vulnerabilities they target. The dossier uses this taxonomy alongside the BITE, Lifton and Langone frameworks to label rhetorical control mechanisms.

    NLP · MethodsACL Anthology

Institutional & investigative reports

Rigorous and widely cited — but not peer-reviewed

Note on statusEpistemic hygiene

These are produced by think-tanks, watchdogs and university programmes. They are carefully researched and form much of the evidentiary base of the main dossier, but they do not undergo academic peer review. They are kept separate so the distinction between peer-reviewed scholarship and institutional research stays explicit. The Critical Review page discusses how to weight these tiers.

  1. 2020Report

    The QAnon Conspiracy: Destroying Families, Dividing Communities, Undermining Democracy

    Network Contagion Research Institute (NCRI) & Rutgers Eagleton Institute

    ReportNCRI2020

    An early influential mapping of QAnon's online growth and offline harms, frequently cited as a bridge between computational measurement and policy concern.

    Contagion · Policy
  2. 2021Report

    Examining Extremism: QAnon

    Daniel Suber & Jacob Ware · CSIS

    ReportCSIS2021

    Documents 22 violent incidents linked to QAnon (2018–Jan 2021) — nine classed as terrorism, three fatal — and traces the platform path 4chan → 8chan → 8kun.

    Extremism studies
  3. 2021Report

    Quantifying the Q Conspiracy: A Data-Driven Approach

    The Soufan Center

    ReportSoufan Center2021

    Found that roughly one-fifth of 166,820 QAnon posts (Jan 2020–Feb 2021) originated abroad — primarily Russia and China — quantifying the foreign-amplification dimension.

    National security
  4. 2022Report

    Crowdfunding the QAnon Movement

    ADL Center on Extremism

    ReportAnti-Defamation League2022

    Traced $6,246,072 raised across 324 campaigns on 10 platforms (2016–mid-2022) — the basis for the dossier's money-trail section.

    Watchdog · Funding
  5. 2023Report

    Understanding Conspiracist Radicalisation

    Anna Kruglova · ICCT

    ReportICCT2023

    Argues QAnon is not exceptional, identifying five recurring discourses — revenge, "the Other", chosenness, apocalypse, urgency/altruism — shared with other radicalising movements.

    Counter-terrorism
  6. 2021+Report

    "This Is Our House!" & the Jan 6 defendant database

    Program on Extremism · George Washington University

    ReportGWU2021 – ongoing

    Ongoing tracking of federal January 6 prosecutions, including the subset with overt QAnon affiliation — the source the dossier uses to correct the widely-repeated "1 in 12" claim.

    Extremism · Court tracking

On peer review. Inclusion is not endorsement of every claim; peer review certifies method and review, not infallibility. Venues span refereed journals (First Monday, Current Sociology, Sociological Perspectives, Terrorism and Political Violence, Democracy and Security, Journal for Deradicalization, Critical Sociology, the Springer religion journal, the Journal of Online Trust and Safety) and competitively-reviewed conferences (ICWSM, The Web Conference, ACM WebSci, CSCW, ACL, CogSci). Two entries are clearly flagged as a monograph (Bloom & Moskalenko) and a terrorism-studies bulletin (CTC Sentinel); the report section is non-peer-reviewed by design. DOIs/arXiv IDs were checked against the publisher or preprint server; where none is shown, the citation is given so the work can be located via a library or scholarly index.

The iDRAMA spine. Track A is reproduced from the lab's own publications index, which links open-access PDFs and datasets for each paper. Their seven-year, multi-platform record is the closest thing the field has to a longitudinal study of QAnon.
