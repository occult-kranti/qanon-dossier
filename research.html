QAnon emerged as an object of serious scholarship almost as fast as it spread. The entries below are ordered by date of publication so the development of the field is visible: early cross-platform measurement work (2020), the consolidation of computational and sociological study (2021–2022), and a turn toward religion, kinship, and manipulation analysis (2023–2024). Each card states the venue, year, and — where verified against the publisher — a DOI or archival link. Institutional reports (CSIS, ICCT, ADL, Soufan, GWU) are rigorous and widely cited but are not peer-reviewed; they are listed in their own section at the end.

2020 — Origins mapped

The first systematic, data-driven studies appear

  1. 2020Paper 01

    Tracing Normiefication: A Cross-Platform Analysis of the QAnon Conspiracy Theory

    Daniël de Zeeuw, Sal Hagen, Stijn Peeters & Emilija Jokubauskaitė · University of Amsterdam (OILab)

    Journal First Monday Vol. 25, No. 11 26 Oct 2020 Peer-reviewed

    The foundational measurement study. The authors trace QAnon-related activity between 28 October 2017 and 1 November 2018 across 4chan, 8chan, Reddit, YouTube and the news, theorising its diffusion as normiefication — the passage of fringe ideas into mainstream platforms. They find a clear incubation period on 4chan /pol/, rapid migration to YouTube and Reddit, and news coverage that only began once the phenomenon moved offline. The paper supplies the chronology and method later work builds on.

    Media studies · Comp. social science doi.org/10.5210/fm.v25i11.10643 First Monday
  2. 2020Paper 02

    QAnon as a Variation of a Satanic Conspiracy Theory: An Overview

    Miroslav Vrzal · Masaryk University

    Journal Theory and Practice in English Studies Vol. 9, No. 1–2, pp. 45–66 2020 Peer-reviewed

    Situates QAnon within the long lineage of satanic-panic narratives — the 1980s–90s satanic ritual abuse scare, blood-libel motifs, and Manichaean "good vs. evil" framing. A useful corrective to treating QAnon as historically unprecedented: it inherits a recognisable conspiratorial grammar, repackaged for the imageboard era.

    Religious studies · Cultural analysis

2021 — The field forms

Computational, cognitive and sociological study consolidate

  1. 2021Paper 03

    "Is it a Qoincidence?": An Exploratory Study of QAnon on Voat

    Antonis Papasavva, Jeremy Blackburn, Gianluca Stringhini, Savvas Zannettou & Emiliano De Cristofaro

    Conference The Web Conference (WWW '21) Proceedings of WWW 2021 2021 Peer-reviewed

    After Reddit's QAnon bans, much of the community decamped to Voat. This is the first large-scale measurement of that space — hundreds of thousands of submissions and comments from /v/GreatAwakening — characterising who posted, what they discussed, and how the discourse compared to a baseline. A core empirical contribution to understanding QAnon's platform diaspora.

    Computer science · Web measurement arXiv:2009.04885
  2. 2021Paper 04

    Characterizing Social Imaginaries and Self-Disclosures of Dissonance in Online Conspiracy Discussion Communities

    Shruti Phadke, Mattia Samory & Tanushree Mitra

    Conference Proc. ACM Human-Computer Interaction (CSCW) Vol. 5, CSCW2 Oct 2021 Peer-reviewed

    Where most work studies belief, this paper studies doubt. It characterises moments when conspiracy participants disclose cognitive dissonance — expressions that may signal who is starting to disengage and under what conditions. Directly relevant to the dossier's recovery sections: it locates the cracks through which people leave.

    HCI · CSCW
  3. 2021Paper 05

    Rise of QAnon: A Mental Model of Good and Evil Stews in an Echochamber

    J. Hunter Priniski, Mason McClay & Keith J. Holyoak · UCLA

    Conference Cognitive Science Society (CogSci) Annual Meeting Proceedings 2021 Peer-reviewed

    A cognitive-science account: QAnon belief is modelled as a coherent good-versus-evil mental model that gains explanatory power as it absorbs new events, reinforced within an echo chamber. Helps explain why disconfirmation (failed predictions) rarely dislodges believers — the model simply re-absorbs the contradiction.

    Cognitive science
  4. 2021Paper 06

    Panic, Pizza and Mainstreaming the Alt-right: A Social Media Analysis of Pizzagate and the Rise of the QAnon Conspiracy

    Paul Bleakley

    Journal Current Sociology (SAGE) Online first 2021 Peer-reviewed

    Traces the direct line from Pizzagate (2016) to QAnon, arguing the earlier panic functioned as a proof-of-concept that helped mainstream alt-right framings. Connects the 2016 Comet Ping Pong shooting to the narrative machinery QAnon would later industrialise.

    Sociology

2022 — Canonical analysis

Studying the drops themselves

  1. 2022Paper 07

    The Gospel According to Q: Understanding the QAnon Conspiracy from the Perspective of Canonical Information

    Antonis Papasavva, Max Aliapoulios, Cameron Ballard, Emiliano De Cristofaro, Gianluca Stringhini, Savvas Zannettou & Jeremy Blackburn

    Conference ICWSM 2022 Proc. 16th Int'l AAAI Conf. on Web & Social Media, 16(1) 735–746 2022 Peer-reviewed

    The most-cited technical study of the drops. The authors build a dataset of 4,949 canonical Q drops collected from six aggregation sites, show the sites disagree on what counts as canonical (so some drops are likely apocryphal), and — analysing writing across the most-used tripcodes — find statistically significant evidence that the drops were not authored by a single individual. They also show the drops themselves are not especially toxic; the violence comes from adherents' interpretations.

    Computer science · Social media doi.org/10.1609/icwsm.v16i1.19330 arXiv:2101.08750

2023 — Religion & deviance

Why it resonates, and what it does to families

  1. 2023Paper 08

    The Revelations of Q: Dissemination and Resonance of the QAnon Conspiracy Theory among US Evangelical Christians and the Role of the Covid-19 Crisis

    Heiko Beyer & Niklas Herrberg · Heinrich Heine University Düsseldorf

    Journal Z. für Religion, Gesellschaft und Politik (Springer) 1 Mar 2023 Peer-reviewed

    An empirical test of why QAnon found purchase among US evangelicals. Using Pew's American Trends Panel (waves 46, 68, 73), the authors argue that biblicism breeds a belief in absolute truth (nomization), a clean good/evil split (Manichaeism), and salvation through politics (immanent eschatology) — and that under COVID uncertainty these resonate with the epistemic, moral and eschatological pulls of conspiracy thinking. Holds when controlling for Trump support.

    Sociology of religion doi.org/10.1007/s41682-023-00147-2 SpringerLink
  2. 2023Paper 09

    "I Would Give Anything to Talk about Aliens Now": QAnon Conspiracy Theories and the Creation of Cognitive Deviance

    Meggan M. Jordan & Jennifer Marie Whitmer

    Journal Sociological Perspectives (SAGE) 2023 Peer-reviewed

    The human cost, studied directly. Through 20 in-depth interviews with adults worried about a family member's QAnon involvement, the authors document epistemic conflict, out-of-character behaviour, broken boundaries and fear of future actions — and develop the concept of cognitive deviance: the point at which beliefs become deviant in the eyes of intimates. Empirical grounding for the dossier's survivor sections.

    Sociology doi.org/10.1177/07311214231181383

2024 — Manipulation, computationally

Tools for analysing manipulative discourse

  1. 2024Paper 10

    MentalManip: A Dataset for Fine-grained Analysis of Mental Manipulation in Conversations

    Yuxin Wang, Ivory Yang, Saeed Hassanpour & Soroush Vosoughi · Dartmouth College

    Conference ACL 2024 Proc. 62nd Annual Meeting of the ACL, pp. 3747–3764 2024 Peer-reviewed

    Not a QAnon paper per se, but the methodological backbone for analysing manipulation as discourse. The authors release a 4,000-dialogue dataset annotated for manipulation techniques and the vulnerabilities they target, and show current language models struggle to detect manipulation reliably. The dossier uses this taxonomy alongside the BITE, Lifton and Langone frameworks to label rhetorical control mechanisms.

    NLP · Computational linguistics ACL Anthology

Institutional & investigative reports

Rigorous and widely cited — but not peer-reviewed

Note on statusEpistemic hygiene

The sources below are produced by think-tanks, watchdogs and university programmes. They are carefully researched and form much of the evidentiary base of the main dossier, but they do not undergo academic peer review. They are kept separate here so the distinction between peer-reviewed scholarship and institutional research stays explicit.

  1. 2021Report

    Examining Extremism: QAnon

    Daniel Suber & Jacob Ware · Center for Strategic & International Studies (CSIS)

    ReportCSIS10 June 2021

    Documents 22 violent incidents linked to QAnon (2018–Jan 2021), including nine classed as terrorism and three fatal — and traces the platform path 4chan → 8chan → 8kun. A primary source for the dossier's violence section.

    Extremism studies
  2. 2021Report

    Quantifying the Q Conspiracy: A Data-Driven Approach

    The Soufan Center

    ReportThe Soufan Center2021

    Found that roughly one-fifth of 166,820 QAnon posts (Jan 2020–Feb 2021) originated abroad — primarily Russia and China — quantifying the foreign-amplification dimension.

    National security
  3. 2022Report

    Crowdfunding the QAnon Movement

    ADL Center on Extremism

    ReportAnti-Defamation League2022

    Traced $6,246,072 raised across 324 campaigns on 10 platforms (2016–mid-2022) — the evidentiary basis for the dossier's money-trail section.

    Watchdog · Funding
  4. 2023Report

    Understanding Conspiracist Radicalisation

    Anna Kruglova · International Centre for Counter-Terrorism (ICCT)

    ReportICCT2023

    Argues QAnon is not exceptional, identifying five recurring discourses — revenge, "the Other", chosenness, apocalypse, and urgency/altruism — shared with other radicalising movements.

    Counter-terrorism
  5. 2021+Report

    "This Is Our House!" & the Jan 6 defendant database

    Program on Extremism · George Washington University

    ReportGWU2021 – ongoing

    Ongoing tracking of federal January 6 prosecutions (now well over 1,500 defendants), including the subset with overt QAnon affiliation — the source the dossier uses to correct the widely-repeated "1 in 12" claim.

    Extremism · Court tracking
  6. 2021Report

    The QAnon Timeline: Four Years, 5,000 Drops and Countless Failed Prophecies

    Bellingcat (open-source investigation)

    InvestigationBellingcat2021

    A quantitative + qualitative chronology of the drops from Oct 2017 to Oct 2020 — a key reference for the Timeline page.

    OSINT journalism

On peer review. Inclusion here is not endorsement of every claim a paper makes; peer review certifies method and review, not infallibility. Venues span refereed journals (First Monday, Current Sociology, Sociological Perspectives, the Springer religion journal) and competitively-reviewed conference proceedings (ICWSM, The Web Conference, CSCW, ACL, CogSci). Where a DOI is shown it was checked against the publisher of record; where no link appears, the citation is given so it can be located via a library or scholarly index.

Purpose. This page exists to point readers toward the primary scholarly record so they can read it directly rather than relying on summaries. It treats QAnon as an object of study, not a viewpoint to amplify.
